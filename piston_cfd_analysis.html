Project 03 · Simulation

CFD Analysis of IC Engine Piston Heads

Reactive-flow simulations comparing Flat-top and Circular Dish combustion performance.

1. The Problem & Objective

In compression-ignition (CI) engines, achieving high thermal efficiency while simultaneously lowering emissions is a major technical hurdle. Conventional flat piston designs often suffer from poor air utilization. The core objective was to evaluate how subtle changes in the piston's bowl lip or bottom curvature optimize the combustion rate without increasing thermal stress.

The bowl shape dictates the swirl and squish motions, which are essential for breaking up the high-pressure fuel spray. Proper geometry centralizes the flame kernel, minimizing heat loss to the cylinder walls, and enhanced turbulence reduces physical delay for more complete combustion.

2. Simulation Setup & Methodology

The numerical framework was built using the ANSYS Forte Sector Mesh generator utilizing Topology 3. We integrated the CHEMKIN-Pro chemistry solver for precise ignition and emission kinetics, which significantly reduces simulation time compared to traditional CFD. The RANS RNG k-epsilon turbulence model was utilized, as it is optimized for high-strain flows and swirling motion in engines.

The simulated engine featured a 10 cm bore and stroke, with a 175 cm connecting rod. Fuel injection was modeled using a centrally located solid injector with a 150-micron nozzle, injecting 30 mg of mass over a 10° crank angle starting at -15° BTDC.

3. Geometry Comparison & Results

We conducted a comparative study between a Flat-top geometry, which relies almost entirely on the intake port for swirl and lacks squish motion, and a Circular Dish-type head, which significantly enhances squish and tumble ratios. The analysis confirmed the superior thermodynamic performance of the dish geometry.

The dish geometry generated a peak turbulence velocity of 400 cm/sec, confirming high-intensity mixing. The simulation achieved a peak pressure of 135 bar and a peak temperature of 1550 K, indicating highly efficient, well-timed heat release. Additionally, a sharp transition in O2 and CO2 mole fractions at -10° CA validated that the dish geometry promotes near-complete combustion with minimal ignition delay.

4. Technical Challenges

The project involved a steep technical learning curve and computational limitations. Simulating complex bowl boundaries initially resulted in recurring solver errors and mesh instabilities, leading us to pivot and focus exclusively on the Flat and Dish-type heads for data reliability.

Furthermore, the high CPU and RAM demands of the CHEMKIN chemistry solvers pushed our hardware limits, restricting the mesh density and number of iterations we could run without access to a High-Performance Computing (HPC) cluster.
