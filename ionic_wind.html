1. Objective & Vision

For my Bachelor Thesis Project (BTP), under the guidance of Dr. Venugopal, I am working on the design and development of an Electrohydrodynamic (EHD) flow control device. The primary vision for this technology is the active flow control of small jets. By utilizing ionic wind—a solid-state airflow generation technique—boundary layers can be manipulated to alter the aerodynamic characteristics of a jet without introducing any moving mechanical parts.

Beyond jet flow control, the project aims to further explore applications in localized, compact thermal management for high-density systems, where traditional fans fall short.

2. The Physics of Ionic Wind

Ionic wind is generated through a phenomenon known as corona discharge. When a high-voltage DC electric field is applied across an asymmetrical electrode pair—such as a sharp emitter (like a pin or sawtooth) and a blunt collector (like a mesh or plate)—the air near the emitter tip becomes ionized. This creates a dense cloud of ions.

As these ions accelerate toward the grounded collector under the Coulomb force, they continuously collide with neutral air molecules in the 'drift region.' This momentum transfer induces a bulk fluid flow. The resulting electrohydrodynamic (EHD) force provides directed thrust, converting electrical energy directly into kinetic energy.

3. Prototype Design & Geometry

Electrode geometry plays a critical role in optimizing the mechanical efficiency and average velocity of the ionic wind. Configurations like sawtooth-plate and sawtooth-mesh arrays produce a highly concentrated electric field at the sharp edges, lowering the onset voltage required for corona discharge.

In the design phase, parameters such as the number of emission teeth and the inter-electrode gap are fine-tuned to maximize the thrust-to-power ratio. Furthermore, introducing aerodynamic modifications—such as leveraging the Coanda effect with diverging walls or adding central holes in mesh electrodes to reduce pressure drop—can significantly amplify the volumetric flow rate while minimizing frictional losses.

4. Flow Control & Future Scope

The absence of moving parts gives EHD thrusters a distinct advantage: they operate silently, possess a unitary structure, and eliminate mechanical wear. While the immediate focus is on manipulating the flow of small jets, the applications extend deeply into advanced thermofluids.

Because ionic wind can drastically enhance convective heat transfer (with some multi-stage configurations improving heat transfer coefficients by over 30%), it is an ideal candidate for cooling miniaturized, high-frequency electronic devices where conventional mechanical fans are impractical. Future iterations will explore multi-stage parallel amplification to scale up the flow velocity for more demanding aerospace implementations.
